Chapter 4: Completing the Accounting Cycle - Summary and End-of-Chapter Materials

Summary for a 4th Grader

Imagine you run a lemonade stand. You sell drinks, buy lemons, and rent a table. At month's end, you check how much money you made. But it's tricky! If you buy lemons for two months but use only half now, don't count all the cost this month—that makes it look like you lost money when you didn't.

Or, if a friend owes you $10 for lemonade you gave today but pays tomorrow, count it as money made now.

This chapter is about fixing your money records (called accounting) to show the true story. Key ideas:

  • Accrual basis: Count money when it's earned or spent, not when cash moves.
  • Deferrals: Paid or got paid early (e.g., prepaid rent or advance payment for work not done).
  • Accruals: Earned or owed something, but no cash yet (e.g., unpaid wages or money owed to you).
  • Depreciation: Stuff like your cart wears out, so spread the cost over time.

Steps: Make adjusting entries to fix records, create an adjusted trial balance to check, prepare financial statements (like income reports), and do closing entries to reset for next month.

This helps see if your stand is really succeeding!

Short Answer Questions

1. LO 4.1 Describe the revenue recognition principle. Give specifics.

The revenue recognition principle requires that revenue be reported when it is earned, regardless of when cash is received. For example, if a company provides services in December but the customer pays in January, the revenue is recognized in December when the services were performed.

2. LO 4.1 Describe the expense recognition principle (matching principle). Give specifics.

The expense recognition principle, or matching principle, requires that expenses be reported in the same period as the revenues they help generate. For example, if inventory is sold in a period, the cost of that inventory (an expense) should be recorded in the same period as the sale revenue, even if the inventory was purchased earlier.

3. LO 4.2 What parts of the accounting cycle require analytical processes, rather than methodical processes? Explain.

Analyzing transactions to determine journal entries requires analytical processes, as it involves deciding which accounts are affected and whether they are debits or credits. This applies to both initial entries and adjusting entries. Other steps, like posting to ledgers, preparing trial balances, and closing entries, are more methodical and rule-based.

4. LO 4.2 Why is the adjusting process needed?

The adjusting process is needed to ensure that financial statements reflect the correct revenues and expenses for the period under accrual accounting. It updates accounts for accruals and deferrals so that the statements comply with the revenue recognition and matching principles, providing a true picture of financial performance and position.

5. LO 4.2 Name two types of adjusting journal entries that are commonly made before preparing financial statements? Explain, with examples.

Two types are accruals and deferrals. Accruals record revenues or expenses that have occurred but not yet been recorded, like accruing salaries payable for work done but not paid (debit Salaries Expense, credit Salaries Payable). Deferrals adjust for payments or receipts in advance, like adjusting prepaid insurance for the portion used (debit Insurance Expense, credit Prepaid Insurance).

6. LO 4.2 Are there any accounts that would never have an adjusting entry? Explain.

Accounts like Cash typically do not have adjusting entries because cash transactions are recorded when they occur, and there are no timing differences to adjust. Equity accounts like Common Stock also rarely need adjustments unless there's a specific error or reclassification.

7. LO 4.2 Why do adjusting entries always include both balance sheet and income statement accounts?

Adjusting entries shift amounts between balance sheet accounts (like assets or liabilities) and income statement accounts (like revenues or expenses) to properly match timing under accrual accounting. For example, accruing an expense increases a liability (balance sheet) and an expense (income statement).

8. LO 4.2 Why are adjusting journal entries needed?

Adjusting journal entries are needed to update account balances for events that have occurred but not yet been recorded, ensuring financial statements are accurate and comply with accrual basis principles. Without them, revenues and expenses might be mismatched across periods.

9. LO 4.3 If the Supplies account had an ending balance of $1,200 and the actual count for the remaining supplies was $400 at the end of the period, what adjustment would be needed?

Debit Supplies Expense $800; Credit Supplies $800. This adjusts the Supplies account to the actual $400 remaining, recognizing the $800 used as an expense.

10. LO 4.3 When a company collects cash from customers before performing the contracted service, what is the impact, and how should it be recorded?

The impact is an increase in cash (asset) and unearned revenue (liability). Record as: Debit Cash; Credit Unearned Revenue. Later, when services are performed, adjust by Debit Unearned Revenue; Credit Service Revenue.

11. LO 4.3 If the Prepaid Insurance account had a balance of $12,000, representing one year’s policy premium, which was paid on July 1, what entry would be needed to adjust the Prepaid Insurance account at the end of December, before preparing the financial statements?

From July 1 to December 31 is 6 months, so half the policy ($6,000) has expired. Debit Insurance Expense $6,000; Credit Prepaid Insurance $6,000.

12. LO 4.3 If adjusting entries include these listed accounts, what other account must be in that entry as well? (A) Depreciation expense; (B) Unearned Service Revenue; (C) Prepaid Insurance; (D) Interest Payable.

(A) Accumulated Depreciation; (B) Service Revenue; (C) Insurance Expense; (D) Interest Expense.

13. LO 4.4 What is the difference between the trial balance and the adjusted trial balance?

The trial balance is prepared before adjustments and lists unadjusted account balances to check if debits equal credits. The adjusted trial balance is prepared after posting adjusting entries and reflects corrected balances used for financial statements.

14. LO 4.4 Why is the adjusted trial balance trusted as a reliable source for building the financial statements?

It incorporates all adjustments for accruals, deferrals, and estimates, ensuring balances align with accrual accounting principles. Debits equal credits, and it directly feeds into the income statement, retained earnings statement, and balance sheet.

15. LO 4.5 Indicate on which financial statement the following accounts (from the adjusted trial balance) would appear: (A) Sales Revenue; (B) Unearned Rent Revenue; (C) Prepaid Advertising; (D) Advertising Expense; (E) Dividends; (F) Cash.

(A) Income Statement; (B) Balance Sheet; (C) Balance Sheet; (D) Income Statement; (E) Retained Earnings Statement; (F) Balance Sheet.
